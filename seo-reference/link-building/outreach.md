## Build Process (Ordered Steps)

1. **Define campaign goals** — Determine target pages for link acquisition, desired anchor text (within safe ratios from anchor_text service), and DR minimums for target sites. Pull competitor gap data from link_analysis if available.

2. **Build prospect list** — Identify target sites from: competitor backlink gap analysis, industry publications, resource pages, blogs covering the client's topics. Filter by DR 30+ minimum, topical relevance, and editorial quality.

3. **Research each prospect** — For each target site, read the 3-5 most recent articles. Identify the author/editor, their writing topics, and a specific content angle that connects to the client's expertise. This is non-negotiable — generic outreach converts at <1%.

4. **Craft personalized emails** — Write initial outreach referencing the prospect's specific recent content. Include a clear value proposition: what the client offers the target site's audience that they don't already have (data, expertise, unique angle).

5. **Build follow-up sequence** — Create 2-3 follow-up emails spaced 4-7 days apart. Each adds new value (different content angle, additional resource, updated data) rather than just "checking in."

6. **A/B test subject lines** — Create 2-3 subject line variants per campaign. Track open rates. Optimize: question format vs. statement, personalized vs. topic-focused, short (<40 chars) vs. descriptive.

7. **Prepare guest post pitches** — Where guest posting is appropriate, draft 3 topic proposals per target site. Each topic must fill a genuine gap in the site's existing coverage and survive SpamBrain content quality analysis.

8. **Set up digital PR assets** — If the campaign includes proactive PR, prepare linkable assets: data studies, survey results, tools, templates, infographics. These attract inbound media interest — reactive PR is table stakes; proactive is premium.

9. **Configure journalist sourcing** — Set up monitoring on HARO (revived by Featured.com April 2025), Featured.com, and Qwoted. Create response templates for relevant journalist queries matching the client's expertise.

10. **Launch and track** — Send campaigns in batches (not all at once). Track opens, replies, links acquired, and conversion rates per template variant.

## Critical Patterns

### 1. Personalization From Target Site's Recent Content Is Mandatory
**WHEN:** Writing any outreach email.
**HOW:** Read the prospect's 3-5 most recent articles. Reference a specific article, quote, or point in the opening sentence. Connect that reference to the client's expertise or content.
**WHY:** Generic outreach converts at <1%. Personalized outreach referencing recent content converts at 5-15%. The ROI difference is 10x.
**DON'T:** Use "I love your blog" or "I've been following your work." Reference a specific article by name with a substantive comment.

### 2. HARO/Featured.com/Qwoted Strategy Is Reactive PR
**WHEN:** Journalist sourcing for link acquisition.
**HOW:** HARO was revived by Featured.com in April 2025, keeping the original free email format. Monitor daily queries. Respond within 2 hours of publication (first responders get picked). Keep responses to 200-300 words with specific data points. Include a professional bio with credentials.
**WHY:** Journalist-sourced links are the gold standard — editorial, high-DR, and SpamBrain-safe. Featured.com offers subscription tiers for priority access.
**DON'T:** Respond to queries outside the client's genuine expertise. Journalists detect padding.

### 3. Proactive Digital PR Beats Reactive
**WHEN:** Building a premium link campaign.
**HOW:** Create linkable assets: original research (survey 500+ people in the industry), data studies (analyze publicly available datasets), interactive tools, comprehensive templates. Then pitch these assets to targeted reporters with personalized story angles.
**WHY:** Reactive PR (responding to journalist queries) is table stakes. Proactive PR (creating newsworthy content that attracts inbound media) is what separates premium agencies.
**DON'T:** Call a blog post "original research." Linkable assets need genuinely proprietary data or tools.

### 4. SpamBrain-Safe Content Quality for Guest Posts
**WHEN:** Drafting guest post content.
**HOW:** Google's October 2025 spam update explicitly targeted AI-generated guest post farms. Every guest post must: provide genuine value to the target site's audience, include original insights (not rewritten search results), and be quality the client would publish on their own site.
**WHY:** SpamBrain evaluates content at the network level. Thin guest posts on multiple sites create a detectable pattern.
**DON'T:** Produce guest posts that are obviously written just for the link. The content must stand on its own merit.

### 5. A/B Tested Subject Lines Drive Open Rates
**WHEN:** Launching any outreach campaign.
**HOW:** Create 2-3 subject line variants. Split test across equal prospect segments. Track: open rate, reply rate, link conversion rate. Winning patterns: question format ("Did you see the gap in [topic]?"), personalized ("[Name], re: your [article topic]"), short and specific (<40 characters).
**WHY:** Subject line determines whether the email gets opened. A 20% vs 40% open rate doubles your entire campaign output.
**DON'T:** Use clickbait, ALL CAPS, or exclamation marks. Spam filters and editors both reject these.

### 6. Follow-Up Sequences Add Value, Not Pressure
**WHEN:** A prospect hasn't responded after initial outreach.
**HOW:** Send 2-3 follow-ups spaced 4-7 days apart. Each follow-up introduces new value: a different content angle, a relevant data point, or a resource the prospect can use regardless of whether they link. Final follow-up offers a clean exit ("No worries if this isn't a fit").
**WHY:** 80% of links are acquired on follow-up 2 or 3. But "just checking in" emails destroy relationships.
**DON'T:** Send more than 4 total touches. After 4 with no response, the prospect isn't interested.

### 7. Anchor Text in Outreach Must Follow Safe Ratios
**WHEN:** Suggesting anchor text for earned links.
**HOW:** When a prospect agrees to link, suggest branded anchor text (company name) or natural phrase anchors. Never request exact-match keyword anchors. Cross-reference with anchor_text service for the client's current distribution.
**WHY:** Requesting exact-match anchors is the fastest way to trigger a SpamBrain penalty. It also signals manipulation to savvy editors.
**DON'T:** Include suggested anchor text in the initial outreach email. Wait until the link is agreed upon, then gently suggest branded anchors.

### 8. Target DR 50+ for Digital PR, DR 30+ for Guest Posts
**WHEN:** Setting campaign quality thresholds.
**HOW:** Digital PR targets (media outlets, news sites): DR 50 minimum. Guest post targets (industry blogs, niche publications): DR 30 minimum. Resource page inclusions: DR 25 minimum with high topical relevance.
**WHY:** Lower-DR links provide diminishing returns and can trigger "low quality network" patterns if overrepresented.
**DON'T:** Chase only DR 80+ sites. A DR 40 niche-relevant editorial link often outperforms a DR 70 generic sidebar link.

### 9. Connect Outreach to Content Strategy
**WHEN:** Planning which content to pitch or create for link acquisition.
**HOW:** Outreach topics should overlap with the client's existing content clusters. If the client publishes about "enterprise SaaS onboarding," outreach should target sites covering that topic.
**WHY:** Topical relevance between linking domain and target domain is a SpamBrain evaluation factor. Random links from unrelated sites look manipulated.
**DON'T:** Pitch content to sites outside the client's topical ecosystem just because they have high DR.

### 10. Track Everything Monthly
**WHEN:** Reporting on outreach campaign results.
**HOW:** Monthly report: emails sent, open rate, reply rate, links acquired, DR of each linking domain, anchor text used, target page linked to, cost per link. Compare against previous months.
**WHY:** Without tracking, you can't optimize. Without optimization, cost per link stays at $750+ instead of improving.
**DON'T:** Report only "links acquired" without quality metrics. 10 DR-20 links are not equivalent to 3 DR-60 links.

## Quality Gate

- [ ] Every outreach email references the prospect's specific recent content (not generic praise)
- [ ] Subject lines have 2-3 A/B variants per campaign segment
- [ ] Follow-up sequences add new value at each touch (not "just checking in")
- [ ] Guest post topics fill genuine gaps in the target site's coverage
- [ ] Guest post content quality would pass SpamBrain editorial analysis
- [ ] Anchor text suggestions stay within safe ratio thresholds
- [ ] Prospect list filtered by DR minimums (30+ guest post, 50+ digital PR)
- [ ] Topical relevance verified between prospect site and client's content areas
- [ ] Digital PR assets contain genuinely proprietary data or tools
- [ ] HARO/Featured.com responses are under 300 words with specific credentials
- [ ] Monthly tracking covers: links acquired, DR, anchor text, cost per link
- [ ] No spam-triggering language in email copy (ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation, clickbait)

### Sample Outreach Emails — GOOD vs. BAD

#### Initial Outreach Email

**GOOD example:**
> **Subject:** Re: your piece on SaaS churn benchmarks
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I just read your January analysis on SaaS churn benchmarks — the breakdown of logo churn vs. revenue churn by company stage was the clearest explanation I've seen. Your point that Series A companies averaging 5-7% monthly logo churn are "actually healthy" challenged my assumption.
>
> We recently completed a 500-company study on onboarding completion rates and their correlation with 90-day churn. The headline finding: companies with structured onboarding sequences see 34% lower churn than those using ad-hoc approaches — and the effect is strongest for the Series A cohort you highlighted.
>
> Would this data be useful for a follow-up to your churn benchmarks piece? Happy to share the full dataset with methodology if you'd like to reference it.
>
> Best,
> James Carter
> VP of Customer Success, Onboard.io

**BAD example:**
> **Subject:** Guest post opportunity / link exchange
>
> Hi,
>
> I love your blog! You have some great content. I've been following your work for a while now.
>
> I was wondering if you'd be interested in publishing a guest post from our team? We can write about any SaaS topic that would be relevant to your audience. We can also link to your site from ours in return.
>
> Let me know if you're interested!
>
> Best regards,
> The Marketing Team

**Why the good version works:** References a specific article by name, quotes a specific insight, connects the pitch to the prospect's existing content, offers genuinely useful data, and comes from a named person with a relevant title.

**Why the bad version fails:** No personalization (could be sent to anyone), "I love your blog" is a red flag for mass outreach, offers a link exchange (SpamBrain signal), comes from a faceless "Marketing Team," and the subject line screams template.

### Sample Subject Line Variants (A/B Tested Style)

| Variant | Type | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| `Re: your piece on SaaS churn benchmarks` | Personalized reference | Feels like a reply to a real conversation; references their content specifically |
| `Sarah — 500-company churn study (unpublished)` | Name + exclusivity | Personal name boosts open rate; "unpublished" creates curiosity |
| `Data gap in your churn analysis?` | Question + value | Questions get opened; implies specific useful information |
| `34% churn reduction — the onboarding factor` | Stat-led | Specific number creates curiosity; implies data-backed content |
| `Quick question about your benchmarks series` | Short + conversational | Under 40 characters; reads like a real email, not a pitch |

**Testing protocol:** Split list evenly across top 2-3 variants. Track open rate, reply rate, and link conversion rate per variant. Run for 50+ sends per variant before declaring a winner.

**Query:** "Looking for experts to comment on how SaaS companies can reduce customer churn in 2026."

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | What To Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "I love your blog" without referencing a specific article | Editors receive hundreds of generic emails daily; instant delete | Reference a specific article by name with a substantive observation |
| Sending identical templates to 500 prospects | <1% conversion rate; damages brand reputation; may trigger email spam filters | Personalize based on recent content; batch by segment with variants |
| Requesting exact-match anchor text in outreach | Triggers SpamBrain; signals manipulation to experienced editors | Suggest branded anchors or let the editor choose naturally |
| Guest posts written as thinly disguised advertisements | October 2025 spam update targets this; gets rejected by quality sites | Write content that stands on its own merit — link is secondary |
| "Just checking in" follow-ups | Provides zero value; annoys the prospect; reduces future response rates | Each follow-up introduces new value (data, different angle, resource) |
| Targeting only high-DR sites regardless of relevance | Topically irrelevant links are a SpamBrain signal; low conversion rate | Prioritize topical relevance alongside DR |
| Responding to every HARO query regardless of expertise fit | Journalists detect padding; wasted effort on low-match queries | Only respond to queries matching genuine client expertise |
| Sending all outreach emails on the same day | Unnatural velocity pattern; overwhelms inbox management; no A/B learning | Stagger sends in batches; track and optimize between batches |
