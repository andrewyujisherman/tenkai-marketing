## Build Process (Ordered Steps)

1. **Create the Single Source of Truth** — Document the exact, authoritative business NAP: legal business name (exactly as registered), complete street address (with suite/unit format decision), primary phone number, website URL, and secondary phone if applicable. This document is the reference for every citation — every character must be intentional.

2. **Audit Tier 1 platforms** — Check NAP accuracy on the five most influential platforms: Google Business Profile, Bing Places, Apple Maps, Yelp, Facebook. These are the primary citation signals for local ranking.

3. **Audit Tier 2 platforms** — Check NAP on general high-authority directories: BBB, Yellow Pages, Foursquare, Hotfrog, Manta, Citysearch, Superpages, Merchant Circle, Angi (formerly Angie's List), Thumbtack.

4. **Audit industry-specific directories** — Identify and check 5-10 directories specific to the client's industry. Examples: Avvo/FindLaw (legal), Healthgrades/Zocdoc (medical), Houzz (home services), TripAdvisor (hospitality), Clutch (tech/agencies). Industry directories carry more weight than general directories.

5. **Check data aggregators** — Verify coverage with the three major data aggregators: InfoGroup (Data.com), Neustar Localeze, and Factual (now Foursquare). Aggregators distribute business data to hundreds of downstream directories. Incorrect aggregator data propagates errors everywhere.

6. **Detect inconsistencies** — Compare every listing against the Single Source of Truth character by character. Flag: name variations ("LLC" included/excluded), address format differences ("Suite 100" vs. "Ste 100" vs. "#100"), phone format differences ("(555) 123-4567" vs. "555-123-4567"), old addresses, wrong phone numbers.

7. **Detect duplicate listings** — Search each platform for multiple listings of the same business. Duplicates split review signals and confuse Google's entity matching. Flag for suppression or merge.

8. **Build correction priority list** — Rank corrections by platform importance: Tier 1 platforms first, aggregators second (they propagate corrections downstream), Tier 2 and industry directories third.

9. **Identify missing citations** — List platforms where the business has no presence. Prioritize: Tier 1 → aggregators → industry-specific → Tier 2 general.

10. **Create submission templates** — For each missing citation, prepare submission-ready content: exact NAP from the Single Source of Truth, business description (adapted to each platform's character limits), categories, hours, and photos.

11. **Establish monitoring cadence** — Set up quarterly re-audits. Data aggregators and directory auto-updates can introduce new inconsistencies. Monitoring catches drift before it impacts rankings.

## Critical Patterns

### 1. The Single Source of Truth Is the Foundation of Everything
**WHEN:** Beginning any citation work.
**HOW:** Create a document with the exact, canonical NAP:
- **Name:** Legal business name, exact capitalization, with or without "LLC"/"Inc." — decide once and be consistent everywhere
- **Address:** Full address with intentional format choices (Suite vs. Ste, Street vs. St., zip+4 or zip only)
- **Phone:** Primary number in chosen format (all listings must match)
- **URL:** Website homepage (http vs. https, www vs. non-www — pick one)

Copy-paste from this document for every submission. Never type NAP from memory.
**WHY:** A single character inconsistency across platforms confuses Google's entity matching. The Source of Truth eliminates human error in submissions.
**DON'T:** Let individual team members submit citations independently. All submissions must copy from the Source of Truth.

### 2. Tier 1 Platforms Before Everything Else
**WHEN:** Prioritizing citation work.
**HOW:** Fix these five platforms first: Google Business Profile, Bing Places, Apple Maps, Yelp, Facebook. These are the most-crawled, most-referenced citation sources. Inconsistencies here have the highest ranking impact.
**WHY:** Tier 1 platforms are the primary data sources that Google cross-references. Getting these right provides more ranking benefit than fixing 20 minor directories.
**DON'T:** Start with obscure directories to pad the citation count. Quality and consistency on Tier 1 outweighs quantity on Tier 3.

### 3. Data Aggregators Propagate Both Accuracy and Errors
**WHEN:** Planning citation coverage.
**HOW:** Submit accurate NAP to the three major aggregators: InfoGroup (Data.com), Neustar Localeze, and Factual (now Foursquare). These feed business data to hundreds of downstream directories automatically.
**WHY:** One accurate aggregator submission can fix or create dozens of downstream citations. One inaccurate aggregator entry can pollute dozens of directories.
**DON'T:** Skip aggregators because they're "behind the scenes." They're the most efficient way to scale citation accuracy.

### 4. Industry-Specific Directories Outweigh General Directories
**WHEN:** Building new citations beyond Tier 1 and Tier 2.
**HOW:** A law firm listing on Avvo/FindLaw is more valuable than a listing on YellowPages. A restaurant on TripAdvisor matters more than a listing on Manta. Identify 5-10 directories specific to the client's industry. Domain authority of the directory is secondary to topical relevance.
**WHY:** Google's relevance algorithms weight industry-specific citations higher because they validate the business's topical niche. Per 2026 research, relevance outweighs DA for citation value.
**DON'T:** Chase high-DA general directories at the expense of lower-DA industry-specific ones. Relevance wins.

### 5. Character-Level Consistency — No Approximations
**WHEN:** Comparing citations against the Source of Truth.
**HOW:** Check character by character. Common inconsistencies: "Suite 100" vs. "Ste 100" vs. "#100" vs. "Suite #100." "Street" vs. "St." vs. "St." Phone: "(555) 123-4567" vs. "555-123-4567" vs. "555.123.4567" vs. "+1-555-123-4567."
**WHY:** Google's entity matching algorithms treat "Suite 100" and "Ste 100" as potentially different businesses. Consistency must be exact, not approximate.
**DON'T:** Accept "close enough" formatting. Decide on one format in the Source of Truth and enforce it everywhere.

### 6. Duplicate Listings Must Be Suppressed
**WHEN:** Auditing existing citations.
**HOW:** Search each platform for the business name and phone number. Duplicates occur from: previous business owners at the address, old business names after rebranding, multiple team members submitting listings, and aggregator-created auto-listings. Claim or suppress all duplicates.
**WHY:** Duplicate listings split review signals, confuse customers, and dilute citation consistency. Google may display the wrong listing in search results.
**DON'T:** Leave duplicates active and assume Google will figure it out. Actively claim and suppress or merge.

### 7. Minimum 10 Consistent Citations for Small Businesses
**WHEN:** Setting citation targets.
**HOW:** For small businesses in non-competitive markets: minimum 10 quality, 100% consistent citations across Tier 1 + Tier 2 + 2-3 industry directories. For competitive markets: 20-40 citations including all relevant industry directories and local business associations.
**WHY:** Research shows businesses with 40+ accurate citations rank 53% higher. But 10 perfect citations beat 40 inconsistent ones. Quality first, quantity second.
**DON'T:** Build 50 citations on obscure directories while Tier 1 platforms are inconsistent. Fix consistency first, then expand.

### 8. Old Addresses Are the Most Common Error
**WHEN:** Investigating NAP inconsistencies.
**HOW:** When a business moves, old addresses persist across dozens of directories — often for years. Check: has the business moved in the last 5 years? If yes, specifically search for the old address across all platforms. Old addresses require active suppression.
**WHY:** An old address citation contradicts the current address citation, creating confusion in Google's entity matching. This is the #1 source of NAP inconsistency.
**DON'T:** Assume citations auto-update when a business moves. They don't. Manual correction across every platform is required.

### 9. Structured Citations vs. Unstructured Mentions
**WHEN:** Counting and evaluating citations.
**HOW:** Structured citations are directory listings with formal NAP fields (Yelp, BBB, etc.). Unstructured citations are mentions of the business on blogs, news sites, or social media. Both count, but structured citations are more impactful because the NAP data is machine-readable.
**WHY:** Structured citations directly feed Google's entity database. Unstructured mentions support authority but are harder for algorithms to parse.
**DON'T:** Ignore unstructured citations entirely — they provide supporting signals. But prioritize structured citations for NAP accuracy work.

### 10. Quarterly Monitoring Catches NAP Drift
**WHEN:** After initial citation cleanup.
**HOW:** Re-audit Tier 1 and Tier 2 platforms quarterly. Data aggregators auto-update directories, sometimes with stale or incorrect data. Users can suggest edits on Google, Yelp, and Apple Maps. Directories merge or change formats.
**WHY:** Citations are not static. Data drift introduces new inconsistencies continuously. Without monitoring, the initial cleanup degrades over time.
**DON'T:** Treat citation building as a one-time project. Ongoing monitoring is required to maintain consistency.

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | What To Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Building citations on 50 obscure directories before fixing Tier 1 | Tier 1 inconsistencies have 10x the ranking impact of Tier 3 listings | Fix Tier 1 platforms first, then expand |
| Typing NAP from memory instead of copy-pasting from Source of Truth | Human memory introduces subtle variations every time | Create Source of Truth; copy-paste only |
| Treating "Suite 100" and "Ste 100" as equivalent | Google's entity matching treats formatting differences as potentially different businesses | Choose one format and enforce it character-for-character |
| Submitting to aggregators without verifying existing data first | If aggregators already have old/wrong data, a new submission may not override it | Check existing aggregator data, correct it, then verify propagation |
| Ignoring industry-specific directories in favor of general ones | Industry directories carry higher relevance weight than generic directories | Identify 5-10 industry-specific directories and prioritize them |
| Building citations once and never monitoring | Data drift introduces new inconsistencies continuously from aggregator updates and user edits | Quarterly re-audits on all Tier 1 and Tier 2 platforms |
| Leaving duplicate listings active | Duplicates split reviews, confuse customers, and dilute citation signals | Detect and suppress/merge all duplicates before building new citations |
| Counting total citations without measuring consistency | 50 inconsistent citations hurt more than 10 consistent ones | Track consistency percentage alongside total count |
