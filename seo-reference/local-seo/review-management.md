## Professional Standard

Review signals are the second-most influential local ranking factor after GBP signals. Premium agencies manage reviews as an ongoing service — not just drafting responses, but actively tracking velocity, benchmarking against competitors, and running Google-compliant solicitation campaigns. A business with 150 reviews at 4.8 stars typically outranks one with 30 reviews at 5.0.

Premium review management delivers:
- Review response templates by sentiment (positive, neutral, negative) with personalization guidelines

## Build Process (Ordered Steps)

1. **Baseline the current review profile** — Pull from local_audit if available, or collect directly: total reviews, average rating, rating distribution (1-5), reviews per month over last 12 months, current response rate, average response time, last review date.

2. **Benchmark against competitors** — For top 3-5 local competitors, collect the same metrics. Build a comparison table. Identify where the client leads and lags. Calculate the velocity gap (reviews/month differential).

3. **Create response templates** — Build template sets for each sentiment tier:
   - **5-star positive:** Thank, personalize with service details, invite return
   - **4-star positive:** Thank, acknowledge the slight gap, offer to improve
   - **3-star neutral:** Thank, acknowledge specific concerns, offer resolution offline
   - **2-star negative:** Empathize, take responsibility, offer to resolve privately
   - **1-star negative:** Professional empathy, no defensiveness, take conversation offline

4. **Define response protocols** — Set target response times: negative reviews within 24 hours, positive reviews within 48 hours. Establish escalation rules for reviews mentioning legal threats, employee names, or factual inaccuracies.

5. **Design solicitation campaign** — Create a Google-compliant review solicitation workflow: individualized requests sent after specific customer interactions, drip timing (1-3 days post-service), direct link to Google review page, no filtering by sentiment (must ask ALL customers).

6. **Document Google policy compliance** — Explicitly list prohibited practices and ensure every element of the campaign avoids them. This documentation protects the client from accidental violations.

7. **Set up velocity tracking** — Establish monthly review count targets based on competitor benchmarking. Track: new reviews per month, average rating of new reviews, response rate, response time.

8. **Create sentiment analysis framework** — Categorize review themes: service quality, wait times, pricing, staff behavior, cleanliness, communication. Identify recurring negative themes that represent genuine service improvement opportunities.

9. **Configure monitoring and alerts** — Set up notifications for new reviews (especially 1-2 star). Negative reviews should trigger immediate response workflows.

10. **Establish monthly reporting** — Track all metrics month-over-month: total reviews, average rating, velocity, response rate, response time, competitor comparison, sentiment trends.

## Critical Patterns

### 1. Google's Prohibited Review Practices Must Be Explicitly Documented
**WHEN:** Designing any review solicitation campaign.
**HOW:** Document and share with the client these prohibited practices:
- **Review gating:** Surveying customers and only directing happy ones to leave Google reviews
- Offering incentives (payment, discounts, free goods/services) for reviews
- Requesting specific content in reviews ("mention our fast service")
- Mass review requests to large groups simultaneously
- Pressuring users while on premises
- Using review kiosks that filter by sentiment before directing to Google

**WHY:** Google can remove offending reviews, issue warnings, or suspend the Business Profile entirely. Enforcement has increased since 2025.
**DON'T:** Assume the client knows these rules. Explicitly document prohibited practices in every review campaign deliverable.

### 2. What IS Allowed — Positive Framing
**WHEN:** Presenting Google-compliant review solicitation options.
**HOW:** Allowed practices include:
- Asking ALL customers to leave a review (regardless of their experience)
- Sending individualized review requests after specific customer interactions (drip campaigns)
- Providing a direct link to the Google review page
- Responding to all reviews (positive and negative)
- Following up with customers who had service interactions
- Displaying "Review us on Google" signage without filtering
- Timing requests 1-3 days after service when the experience is fresh

**WHY:** Clients need to know what they CAN do, not just what they can't. Positive framing drives action.
**DON'T:** Make the compliance section so fear-inducing that the client stops soliciting reviews entirely. Emphasize what's allowed.

### 3. Response Templates Must Be Personalized, Not Copy-Pasted
**WHEN:** Responding to any review.
**HOW:** Templates provide structure, not exact text. Every response must reference something specific from the review: the service received, the customer's name, a detail they mentioned. "Thank you for your kind words, [Name]! We're glad the [specific service] exceeded your expectations."
**WHY:** Copy-paste responses signal to both Google and future customers that the business doesn't genuinely engage. Personalized responses build trust and encourage more reviews.
**DON'T:** Use the exact same response text for multiple reviews. Google can detect template repetition.

### 4. Negative Review Response — Never Defensive
**WHEN:** Responding to 1-2 star reviews.
**HOW:** Follow the framework: (1) Thank them for the feedback, (2) Empathize with their experience without admitting liability, (3) Briefly state what you've done or will do, (4) Take the conversation offline ("Please contact [name] at [phone/email] so we can make this right"). Never argue, never blame the customer, never dispute facts publicly.
**WHY:** Future customers read negative review responses more carefully than positive ones. A professional response to a negative review can actually increase trust.
**DON'T:** Get defensive, dispute the reviewer's account publicly, or offer compensation in the review response (this attracts complaint-for-compensation behavior).

### 5. Review Velocity Is the New Volume
**WHEN:** Setting review goals for the client.
**HOW:** Track reviews per month, not just total count. Post-March 2026, Google weighs recency more heavily. A business getting 10 reviews/month beats one with 500 total reviews but only 1/month currently. Set monthly velocity targets based on competitor benchmarking.
**WHY:** The 2026 algorithm update elevated review recency above raw volume. Stale review profiles (no new reviews in 30+ days) lose ranking power.
**DON'T:** Report "you have 200 reviews" as if the job is done. Track and target monthly velocity.

### 6. 24-48 Hour Response Target Is Non-Negotiable
**WHEN:** Setting response time standards.
**HOW:** Negative reviews: respond within 24 hours. Positive reviews: respond within 48 hours. Set up mobile notifications for new reviews. Establish an escalation path: if the primary responder can't respond in time, a backup takes over.
**WHY:** Response speed signals to Google (and customers) that the business is actively engaged. Slow responses to negative reviews let the damage compound as more people see an unaddressed complaint.
**DON'T:** Batch review responses weekly. By the time you respond to a negative review 7 days later, dozens of potential customers have seen it unaddressed.

### 7. Review Solicitation Timing Matters
**WHEN:** Designing the drip campaign.
**HOW:** Send review requests 1-3 days after service completion. The experience is fresh enough to recall details but enough time has passed to confirm satisfaction. For service businesses: after the job is complete and the customer has confirmed satisfaction. For retail: 2-3 days after purchase.
**WHY:** Immediate requests feel pressured. Requests weeks later get low response rates because the experience is distant.
**DON'T:** Send review requests on the same day as service. Customers haven't had time to evaluate the outcome.

### 8. Competitor Review Benchmarking Sets Realistic Targets
**WHEN:** Setting velocity and volume goals.
**HOW:** Pull review metrics for top 3-5 local competitors: total reviews, average rating, reviews per month, response rate. Calculate: how many reviews/month does the client need to match the top competitor within 6 months? 12 months?
**WHY:** "Get more reviews" is useless advice without context. Knowing the competitor has 280 reviews growing at 15/month while the client has 45 growing at 3/month creates specific, motivating targets.
**DON'T:** Set arbitrary targets ("get 50 reviews this quarter") without competitive context.

### 9. Sentiment Analysis Identifies Service Improvements
**WHEN:** Analyzing the full review corpus.
**HOW:** Categorize review themes across all reviews: service quality, wait times, pricing, staff behavior, facility quality, communication, scheduling. Identify patterns in negative reviews — recurring complaints represent genuine service improvement opportunities.
**WHY:** Reviews are the most honest customer feedback channel. Pattern analysis turns complaints into actionable business intelligence beyond just SEO.
**DON'T:** Treat sentiment analysis as purely an SEO exercise. Share service improvement insights with the client's operations team.

### 10. Review Response Includes Keywords Naturally
**WHEN:** Crafting review responses.
**HOW:** Naturally incorporate the business's primary services and location in responses: "We're proud to be [City]'s trusted [service type]" or "Our [specific service] team works hard to deliver results." Never force keywords — they must flow naturally.
**WHY:** Review responses are indexed content on the GBP listing. Natural keyword inclusion reinforces local relevance signals.
**DON'T:** Keyword-stuff review responses. "Thank you for choosing our Chicago plumbing emergency plumber Chicago IL services" reads as spam.

## Concrete Examples

### Sample Review Responses — GOOD vs. BAD

#### 5-Star Review Response

**The review:**
> "Robert and his team replaced our water heater in under 3 hours. They were on time, cleaned up after themselves, and the price was exactly what they quoted. Best plumber we've ever used." — Jennifer M.

**GOOD response:**
> Jennifer, thank you for trusting Greenfield Plumbing with your water heater replacement! Robert takes a lot of pride in matching actual work to quoted prices — no surprises is our standard, not a bonus. Glad the team left your utility room cleaner than they found it (that's a Robert rule). If you ever need anything — even just a question about your new unit's maintenance schedule — give us a call at 503-555-0198. We're always here.

**BAD response:**
> Thank you for your 5-star review! We appreciate your business and look forward to serving you again. Please recommend us to your friends and family!

**Why the good version works:** References specific details from the review (water heater, Robert by name, cleaned up, price matching). Adds personality ("that's a Robert rule"). Offers continued value (maintenance schedule question). Feels like a real person wrote it.

**Why the bad version fails:** Could be copy-pasted on any review for any business. No personalization. The "recommend us to friends" ask feels transactional. Google can detect identical templated responses across reviews.

---

#### 3-Star Review Response

**The review:**
> "They fixed the leak but it took two visits because they didn't have the right part the first time. Work quality was good once they got to it." — Mike T.

**GOOD response:**
> Mike, thank you for the honest feedback. You're right — needing a second visit because we didn't have the right part on-hand isn't the experience we aim for. We've since updated our truck inventory for your area to include the fitting type your system needed (it's a less common size, but that's not your problem to solve). The fix itself should be solid for years to come, but if anything comes up, call us directly at 503-555-0198 and ask for David — he'll prioritize getting it handled in one trip.

**BAD response:**
> We're sorry to hear about your experience. We strive for excellence and regret that we fell short. We hope to serve you better next time.

**Why the good version works:** Acknowledges the specific issue without excuses. Explains what changed because of the feedback (updated truck inventory). Provides a named contact for future service. Shows the business actually learned something.

**Why the bad version fails:** Generic apology template. "We strive for excellence" is corporate filler. Doesn't address the actual problem (missing part). Doesn't explain what will be different next time.

---

#### 1-Star Review Response

**The review:**
> "Terrible experience. Technician was 2 hours late, tracked mud through my house, and the repair didn't even fix the problem. I had to call someone else to actually get it done. Never using them again." — Karen D.

**GOOD response:**
> Karen, this is not acceptable, and I take full responsibility. A 2-hour delay without communication, leaving a mess, and an incomplete repair — that's three failures in one visit, and I understand your frustration.
>
> I'd like to make this right. Would you be willing to speak with me directly? I'm Robert Greenfield, the owner — you can reach me at 503-555-0198 or robert@greenfieldplumbing.com. I want to understand exactly what happened and, at minimum, ensure you're not paying for a repair that didn't work.
>
> Your feedback is helping us identify where our process broke down, and I take that seriously.

**BAD response:**
> We're sorry you feel that way. Our technicians are highly trained and we stand behind our work. The delay may have been caused by a previous job running over, which is sometimes unavoidable. If you'd like to discuss further, please call our office.

**Why the good version works:** Takes responsibility without deflecting. Acknowledges each specific complaint (late, mess, incomplete repair). Offers direct contact with the owner by name — signals genuine accountability. Addresses the money issue proactively. Future customers reading this see a business that owns its mistakes.

**Why the bad version fails:** "Sorry you feel that way" is a non-apology that implies the customer is the problem. Defending the technician and making excuses ("previous job running over") publicly contradicts the customer. "Call our office" is impersonal compared to a named owner offering direct contact. Future customers reading this see a business that gets defensive.

**Sent 2 days after service completion. This template is Google-compliant because it asks ALL customers (not filtered by satisfaction), offers no incentive, and doesn't request specific content.**

> **Subject:** How did we do, Jennifer?

>

>
> **[Leave a Google Review →]**
> (link: https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=ChIJN1t_tDeuEmsRUsoyG83frY4)
>
> It only takes about 60 seconds. Whether your experience was great, okay, or something went wrong — we want to hear it.
>
> If anything about the installation isn't right, please call us first at 503-555-0198 so we can fix it. Our work is guaranteed for 2 years.
>
> Thank you,
> Robert Greenfield
> Greenfield Plumbing & Heating

**Compliance notes:**
- Sent to ALL customers after service, not filtered by satisfaction survey (no review gating)
- No incentive offered (no discount, no gift, no entry in a drawing)
- Does not request specific content ("mention our fast service" would violate policy)
- Encourages honest feedback regardless of sentiment ("great, okay, or something went wrong")
- Provides an alternative path for complaints (call us first) — this is allowed and smart, but does NOT filter who sees the Google review link

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | What To Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Review gating (filtering unhappy customers before directing to Google) | Explicitly prohibited by Google; risks profile suspension | Ask ALL customers to review; use the feedback to improve service |
| Offering discounts or free items for reviews | Prohibited; Google removes incentivized reviews; creates legal exposure | Ask for honest reviews without any incentive |
| Copy-pasting identical responses across reviews | Google detects template repetition; customers notice and lose trust | Use templates as structure but personalize every response |
| Responding defensively to negative reviews | Future customers read responses; defensiveness suggests the complaint is valid | Empathize, take responsibility where appropriate, move to offline resolution |
| Targeting only total review count as the goal | 2026 algorithm weighs recency above volume; a stale high-count profile loses to active low-count | Track and target monthly velocity alongside total count |
| Batching review responses weekly | Negative reviews sit unaddressed for days; signals disengagement to Google and customers | Respond to negative reviews within 24 hours, positive within 48 |
| Setting arbitrary review targets without competitor context | "Get 50 reviews" is meaningless if the top competitor has 500 and gains 20/month | Benchmark against competitors; set velocity targets relative to the field |
| Keyword-stuffing review responses | "Thank you Chicago best plumber Chicago IL" reads as spam; damages brand perception | Incorporate location and services naturally in conversational responses |
